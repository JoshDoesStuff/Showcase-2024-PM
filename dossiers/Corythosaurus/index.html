Corythosaurus

Short Introduction

The Corythosaurus, or more specifically, the Corythosaurus Casuarius, was a Herbivorous Dinosaur that lived around 77-78 million years ago.[1] Most of them lived in what is now West America, but remains have been found in other places. Its name was derived from the Greek word κόρυς, which means helmet.

Behaviors and Breeding

Based on its skeletal structure, the Corythosaurus was bipedal, (Stood upright and walked on two legs), and used its tail as a balance while getting food from trees. [2] The Corythosaurus often traveled in groups in order to protect themselves from predators. If a danger got too close to the group, a warning call would be sent to the other members of the group.[3] As for reproduction, the Corythosaurus had a Oviparous birth, or they hatched out of eggs. [3] In order to successfully mate, they need a partner. Presumably, the Corythosaurus used their hollow crest to make a noise in hopes of attracting a partner.[4] The time it takes for the Corythosaurus to mature is unknown, but their average lifespan is estimated to be around 65 years.

Predators

The only 2 known predators to the Corythosaurus were the Gorgosaurus Libratus and an unnamed species of Daspletosaurus.[5] The Gorgosaurus is a member of the Tryannasourids family, and is as dangerous as the T-Rex. For more information on the Gorgosaurus, please navigate to here. Not much is known about the species of Daspletosaurus.

Discovery

The first person to discover the Corythosaurus Casuarius was Barnum Brown in 1911.[6] The first fossil was found in the Red Deer River of Alberta.[6] This find was very interesting because there was most of the skeleton intact, some impressions of skin was still there as well. [6]

Location and Habitat

The Corythosaurus mostly lived in what is currently West America, but many others lived all over North America. There were often groups living in what is now Montana (US), and Alberta (Canada).[3] Since the Corythosaurus was a Herbivore, their habitat was mostly made of trees and other vegetation.

Fossils

The earliest fossil discovery was in 1911 by Barnum Brown, at Red Deer River. [6] With the first skeleton, some impressions of skin were still found. The only missing parts of the newly discovered skeleton were the last 2 feet of the tail, and the front legs. [6] It is presumed that the tail and front legs eroded or weathered away. [6]
