Dilophosaurus

The Dilophosaurus was around on average 8m (23ft), weighing in at about 400 kg (880lbs), and had a skull around 24in. It was slender and built lightly for quick movement, and was the largest land animal predator at its time. Its size is comparable to that of a brown bear or polar bear.[1]

Habitat

Dilophosaurus lived in what is now Arizona, and during its life it is predicted that the environment was warm and wet seasonally, with dry sand dunes coming and going. The average temperature in the Jurassic period was around 5-10°C warmer than it is today, with 4x the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. The excess carbon dioxide resulted in these warmer temperatures because of its greenhouse effect.[2]

Distribution

The only known fossils have been found in the Kayenta Formation and Navajo County, Arizona. While just three fossils have only been found in this region up until now, Dilophosaurus may have lived in a larger area in North America. [3]

Time Period

The Dilophosaurus lived in the early Jurassic period, which began at the end of the Triassic period around 201 million years ago, and ended at the beginning of the Middle Jurassic period, around 175 million years ago. Dinosaurs that lived at the same time as the Dilophosaurus include Ammosaurus, Barapasaurus, Kotasaurus, Lesothosaurus, Lophostropheus, Sarcosaurus, Vulcanodon, and Yunnanosaurus. [4]

Predators

The dilophosaurus was the apex predator of the Early Jurassic period, there were no animals that were above it in the food chain.[5]

Behaviors and Adaptations

The Dilophosaurus was extremely active and bipedal. Its legs were very powerful and the dilophosaurus was capable of fast running, and it had a strong tail as well. The Dilophosaurus’ pelvis was somewhat weak, at first being interpreted as an adaptation for a life in the water, but the strength and proportions of its body didn’t point to an aquatic lifestyle. Now, the widely accepted theory was that the Dilophosaurus used its strong tail to bounce on while attacking an enemy, like how a kangaroo does.[6]

Breeding

Very little is known about the breeding behavior of the dilophosaurus, but it is assumed that one reason for the two crests on top of its head was for attracting mates. As most reptiles do, the dilophosaurus reproduces by laying eggs, and they hatch into a juvenile dilophosaurus, then growing up into an adult.[7]

Fossil Description

The Dilophosaurus was approximately 7m (23ft) in length, with a skull at around 2ft. It had 10 neck vertebrae, 14 back vertebrae, and 45 tail vertebrae. Its large hind legs allowed it to move quickly and catch prey effectively.[8]

Fossil Discovery

A Navajo man in Arizona named Jesse Williams discovered 3 dilophosaurus fossils in 1940. All 3 fossils were in a triangle, the first one almost completely gone, making it worthless. The other two though, were in really good condition. The second fossil had all of its bones intact besides its skull. Luckily, the third fossil had an intact skull, so paleontologist named Charles L. Camp came to excavate shortly after in 1942 and could construct a full dilophosaurus skeleton at UC Berkeley.[9]

Fossil Record

Dilophosaurus fossils have been found in Arizona, three different skeletons in total. Navajo man Jesse Williams discovered these fossils in 1940, and a paleontologist named Charles L. Camp came to excavate shortly after in 1942. The three were found situated in a triangle, around 20 ft apart from each other.[10]
