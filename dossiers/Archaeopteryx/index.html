Archaeopteryx

Overview

The Archaeopteryx, discovered in 1863 by Richard Owen, was one of the first animals to fly upon the planet. Living in the Mesozoic Era (255-66 million years ago), it was briefly believed to have been the first animal to fly, until the soon-after discovery of other prehistoric birds in northern China, Argentina, and Chile. Some other winged dinosaurs discovered to have existed in the same time as the Archaeopteryx are Heperornis regalis and Ichthyornis dispar.1

Food and Diet

The Archaeopteryx had a diet that consisted mainly of other small
creatures and animals. It was a carnivore, so it preyed on fish, small
amphibians, reptiles, smaller mammals, and insects. Although it had
wings, it used them only for gliding, and could not sustain flight like most
modern birds. It used this gliding to scout out prey, dive into water to
catch fish, and swoop in to grab smaller animals it found. While the
Archaeopteryx had a seemingly large array of prey, it was not the top of the
food chain, however. It was a target prey for numerous land and ground dinosaurs.

Habitat

The Archaeopteryx is found to have inhabited much of what is modern
day Bavaria, Germany (South-East Germany). During the times of the
Archaeopteryx, Europe was much closer to the equator, laying at about
the same latitude as modern day Florida. It thrived in a warm and dry climate,
full of rivers and forests where it caught most of its prey. Unlike nearly all modern
birds, the Archaeopteryx is not believed to have ever made nests in
or lived in trees, despite the fact it laid eggs like modern birds.

Discovered

The Archaeopteryx was originally discovered in 1861 in Germany,
where a feather was found. Shortly after, in 1863, a more complete
fossil of the bird's skeleton was discovered in the Bavaria region
of Germany by Richard Owen.

Identification

The prehistoric “bird” had black feathers and small wings
used for gliding. It also had a bony tail which
it used for stability; only three fingers with deadly claws at the
ends, and a beak full of many sharp teeth. Unlike most modern
birds, it did not eat seeds, but rather other small animals and fish.
The Archaeopteryx was relatively small for a dinosaur - ranging
from about the size of a blue-jay or crow - up to the size of a chicken.
