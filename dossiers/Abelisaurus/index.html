Abelisaurus

Classification of Abelisaurus

Abelisaurus is a genus of predatory Abelisaurid Theropod dinosaur that lived during the Late Cretaceous Period (Campaina) of what is now South America. It's a carnivorus dinosaur. It lived in 75-70 million years ago. The genus was named by Jose Bonaparte and Fenando Emilio Novas in 1985, and the type species is Abelisaurus comahuensis. Abelisaurus belongs to the family Abelisauridae, subfamily Camnofaurinae, and infraorder Ceratosauria. It is classified under the suborder large Theropods, class Reptilia, kingdom Animalia, phylum Chordata, order Saurischia, and superorder Dinosauria.

Time (era) of Abelisaurus live in

Where It lived

Abelisaurus is a type of dinosaur that lived during the Late Cretaceous Period, specifically in the Campanian stage or forest, which lasted from approximately 75 to 70 million years ago. During this period, the Earth was warm and humid, with high sea levels and a tropical climate.

Feeding and Diet

Meat-eater

Abelisaurus probably ate large sauropod dinosaurs. It may also have hunted smaller plant-eating dinosaurs. Abelisaurus was a meat-eater and probably hunted other dinosaurs for food.

Scale

Size of Abelisaur

Although only one partial skull of Abelisaurus has been found, it is estimated that the dinosaur was between 7 to 9 meters (25 to 30 feet) long and weighed roughly 3 to 4 tons. The size of the dinosaur is uncertain as it is known from only one partial skull. In 2016, a comprehensive of Abelisaur size estimated its length to be 7 to 9 meters (24 feet - 30 feet). The skull length was estimated at 24 inches in 1985.

Distribution

The dinosaur was discovered in Argentina, in an area known as Patagonia . The fossil was found in the Anacleto Formation between 83 and 80 million years ago . Locally Abelisaurus is known to have come from the Sr. Fernandez field, which is in the Campanian Allen Formation, which makes its stratigraphic position unclear . Most abelisaurids were found in Madagascar, Asia, or sometimes in Africa. Abelisauridae thrived during the Cretaceous period, on the ancient southern supercontinent of Gondwana, and today their fossil remains are found on the modern continents of Africa and South America.

Discovered

Abelisaurus fossils were discovered by Argentine paleontologists José Bonaparte and Fernando Emilio Novas in 1985. The type species, Abelisaurus comahuensis, was named by Roberto Abel. The fossil was found in the Anacleto Formation of Rio Negro Province, Argentina. The skull of Abelisaurus is incomplete, especially on the right side, and most of the connections between the snout and the back of the skull are absent. Despite this, it could be estimated to be over 85 centimeters long.

Behaviors and Adaptions

It is believed to have been an aggressive predator that was skilled at taking down large prey. Paleontologists think that they may have hunted in packs because several individuals had been discovered in the same location. Abelisaurus probably used its sharp teeth and long, narrow head to slice and tear chunks of flesh from their victims. It is also possible that they may have scavenged for food when necessary.

Fossil Description

It consists of a skull, lacking the lower jaws, that is incomplete, especially on the right side. Most of the connections between the snout and the back of the skull are absent. It is also missing most of the palate (roof of the mouth). It had a large head with small teeth and openings in its skull above the jaws. The holes, called “fenestrae,” were likely evolved to lighten the weight of this dinosaur’s massive head, which otherwise might have unbalanced its entire body. Abelisaurus was bipedal and had fairly short arms.
