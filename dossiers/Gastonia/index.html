Description

This dinosaur was around 5 meters to 6 meters long from head to tail and walked on all fours. Gastonia also weighed around a ton when a fully grown adult.[1] Gastonia was armored with a sacral shield and had shoulder spikes for protection on all sides of its back but the total number of spikes on the dinosaur is unknown.[5] The top half of their body (around the head area) had spikes going down from head to tail while getting smaller and smaller the farther down the back and getting duller and duller in the same way until the end of the tail. Gastonia's could headbutt other animals or things because of a mobile brain case for shock absorbance if needed for self defense.

Behaviors and Survival

Gastonias were known to be herding animals and walk with groups of others of the same dinosaur. Gastonia's were also known to be relatively peaceful. Gastonias are herbivores and eat food like leaves, twigs, fruits, and nuts.[8] When it came to communication, the Gastonia would use special sounds or gestures to communicate with other dinosaurs or mates. Speaking of mates, Gastonias would breed and then incubate their eggs until hatched, this sometimes led to the Gastonia being left behind the pack to watch their eggs or sometimes leaving their eggs. [7]

Predators and Threat to Life

Gastonia's threat to life was natural disasters and their main predator, the Utahraptor. The Utahraptor would have had to attack the Gastonia for it to flip over on its belly, due to the belly would have no armor or protection and jumping on its back would be not ideal with all the spikes on its back. This would be the only way to get the kill of a Gastonia although the Gastonia would not try to attack because of its stubby legs. Gastonias could defend themselves by headbutting or using their tail to lash at predators. Gastonia could also run away if need be. [1]

Fossils

The fossils were found about Gastonia in 1998 in Grand County, Utah by James Kirkland. He found said fossils in Yellow Cat Quarry named after Robert Gaston (the founder of the genus). The Gastonia is a very well preserved species and allows for very accurate and easy reconstruction of its body using fossils. It was the first fossil to be mounted in the Denver Museum of Nature and Science. The full body fossil has spikes on its spine and a giant plate located on its lower back (near the tail area). The texture of the fossil was more bumpy and rough, kinda like a rock.[5]

When and Where

Gastonia was a dinosaur in the Early Cretaceous era along with the Triceratops, Irritator and Mosasaurs. It was mostly found living in woodlands, dense forest areas, or swaplands usually with rivers and a lot of brush, in North America. Gastonia would prefer to live near water. This dinosaur lived around what's now called, modern day Utah. [7]
